Her story

Why she wrote this book

Immaculate grew up in Gulu and moved to Kampala for university, where a broken-down car turned into a years-long habit of taking boda-bodas everywhere instead. Riding the same routes for months, she started noticing patterns other passengers didn't — the fuel bought on credit, the informal savings groups run out of a stage chairman's notebook, the mobile money transfers that moved faster than any bank.

Trained as an economist but frustrated by how little formal research reached the people actually living the numbers, she left a research-consulting job in 2022 to report this book full time. She spent three years riding pillion across Kampala, Gulu, and Mbale, interviewing riders, stage chairmen, savings-group treasurers, and the mobile money agents who sit at the edge of almost every stage.

The result is a book written for a general reader — no jargon, no assumed economics degree — but grounded in the kind of on-the-ground reporting that took years to gather.

"Nobody was writing down what I was seeing every single day on the back of a boda. So I decided to write it down myself." — Immaculate N. Aber
How the book came together

A three-year timeline

  1. 2022
    Leaves consulting to report full time
    Starts riding the same Kampala stages daily, taking notes instead of just notes to self.
  2. 2023
    Expands reporting to Gulu and Mbale
    Travels upcountry to test whether Kampala's patterns hold in smaller cities.
  3. 2024
    First full draft
    Three hundred interviews later, the manuscript takes shape around six core chapters.
  4. 2026
    OWN THE BUYER is published
    Self-published in paperback and ebook, sold directly from this site.
